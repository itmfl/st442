  • Q1: What is the most accurate way to compute the average life expectancy for each continent in 1952 ?
## (a) 
gapminder %>% filter(year == 1952) %>% summarise(avg_life_Exp = mean(lifeExp))
    
## (b)
gapminder %>% filter(year == 1952) %>% mutate(total_life = lifeExp*pop) %>% 
                  summarise(avg_life_Exp = mean(total_life))

## (c)
gapminder %>% group_by(continent) %>% filter(year == 1952) %>% 
  mutate(total_life = lifeExp*pop) %>%
  summarise(total_life = sum(total_life), total_pop = sum(pop)) %>% 
  mutate(avg_life_Exp = total_life/total_pop)

## (d)
gapminder %>% filter(year == 1952) %>% group_by(continent) %>% 
  summarise(avg_life_Exp = mean(lifeExp))

Answer (c) as we need to compute the weighted average (as the population for each country differs)

Part 4 (Basic statistics and linear models)

For this part, read the following description. DrinkDrankDrunkis a beverage company. The company is interested in finding the effect of choice of beverages on employees productivity. The company performs a study in which the predictor variable \(X\) is the amount of coffee (in pint) consumed by an employee per day for a duration of one month, \(Z\) is the amount of soda (in pint) consumed by an employee per day for a duration of one month, and \(W\) is the amount of energy drink (in pint) consumed by an employee per day for a duration of one month. The response variable \(Y\) is a measure of productivity of the employee for the month. The number of data points is \(n = 40\). The values of the predictor variable \(X\) ranges from \(1\) to \(5\), the values of the predictor variable \(Z\) ranges from \(1\) to \(4\) and the values of the predictor variables \(W\) ranges from \(0\) to \(3\). A snippet of the data is as follows

## # A tibble: 40 x 4

##    <dbl> <dbl> <dbl> <dbl>
##  1  14.3  2.44     1     5
##  2  14.4  2.48     1     5
##  3  14.4  2.66     1     5
##  4  14.9  2.51     1     5
##  5  14.8  2.51     1     5
##  6  14.5  2.67     1     5
##  7  14.5  2.55     1     5
##  8  14.3  2.37     1     5
##  9  14.6  2.43     1     5
## 10  14.4  2.46     1     5
## #  with 30 more rows

The design matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) satisfies

\[\mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & X_1 & Z_1 & W_1 \\ 1 & X_2 & Z_2 & W_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & X_n & Z_n & W_n \end{bmatrix}; \quad (\mathbf{X}^{T} \mathbf{X})^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 12.18 & 0.28 & -2.65 & -2.15 \\ -0.28 & 3.2 & -0.06 & -1.64 \\ -2.65 & -0.06 & 0.58 & 0.47 \\ -2.15 & -1.64 & 0.47 & 1.18 \end{bmatrix}\]

The following output are the \(95\%\) confidence interval obtained from R for fitting the model \(Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_W W_i + \beta_X X_i + \beta_Z Z_i + \epsilon_i\) to the data where the \(\epsilon_i\) are assumed i.i.d. \(N(0, \sigma^2)\).

mod <- lm(Y ~ W + X + Z, df)
confint(mod, level = 0.95)
##              2.5 % 97.5 %
## (Intercept) 0.3965  3.150
## W           0.6285  2.038
## X           0.0196  0.623
## Z           1.3853  2.243

From the above ouput, answer the following question.

Q1. What is the least square estimate for \(\hat{\beta}_W\) ?

\[\mathrm{(a)} 1.333; \quad \mathrm{(b)} 1.833; \quad \mathrm{(c)} 0.833; \quad \mathrm{(d)} 0.667\]

Answer: \(\hat{\beta}_W\) is the mid-point of the \(95\%\) confidence interval for \(\beta_W\).

Q2. What is a reasonable estimate of \(\sigma^2\) for this data (assuming the posited model is correct) ? You can assume that the \(97.5\%\) quantile for the appropriate Student-\(t\) distribution is \(2\). \[\mathrm{(a)} 1.038; \quad \mathrm{(b)} 0.195; \quad \mathrm{(c)} 0.038; \quad \mathrm{(d)} 0.019\]

Answer: An estimate of \(\sigma^2\) is the MSE. We note that the length of the \(95\%\) confidence interval for say \(\beta_W\) is given by \(2 \times c_{0.975} \times \sqrt{MSE} \times \sqrt{1.18}\) where \(1.18\) is the diagonal entry of \((\mathbf{X}^{\top} \mathbf{X})^{-1}\) corresponding to \(W\). Thus, \(\mathrm{MSE}\) in this case is 0.038.

Q3. What is a reasonable prediction interval for \(Y_*\) when \(\boldsymbol{x}_* = (W_*, X_*, Z_*) = (2,3,4)\) ? (This is a fill-in a blank question. You dont have to provide the exact numbers but you need to demonstrate that you know how to obtain the answer from the output given above).

Answer The prediction interval is simply \[\hat{\beta}_0 + 2 \hat{\beta}_W + 3 \hat{\beta}_X + 4 \hat{\beta}_Z \pm 2 \times \sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}} \times \sqrt{1 + \boldsymbol{x}_*^{\top} (\mathbf{X}^{\top} \mathbf{X})^{-1} \boldsymbol{x}_*}\] where \(\boldsymbol{x}_* = (1, X_*, Z_*, W_*) = (1,3,2,4)\).

Q4. Can you conclude, from the above output, that (assuming the model is true), there is scant evidence supporting the hypothesis that \(\beta_X = 0.65\) and \(\beta_Z = 1\) simultaneously (with \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_W\) arbitrary) ? Why or why not ?

Answer The marginal confidence intervals suggests that \(\beta_X\) is unlikely to be \(0.65\) and \(\beta_Z\) is unlikely to be \(1\). However, \((0.65,1)\) could potentially lie in the joint confidence region for \(\beta_X\) and \(\beta_Z\), but it is hard, without a computer, to see how the joint confidence region for \(\beta_X\) and \(\beta_Z\) looks like using only the above output.
